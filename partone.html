Geologic History

Florida is surrounded by water, and the ocean has had a major impact in molding the geological features of the state. The peninsula we recognize today is only 1/3 of the geologic structure of Florida. Rising and falling sea levels have shaped the different geologic layers over the years.

Warm, shallow water that is clear of sand and sediment created habitat for coral, algae, plankton and other invertebrates. The buildup of skeletal remains from these creatures would form large limestone deposits for over a hundred million years, thus creating a karst geologic layer.

Limestone is porous and easily dissolved by acids that form naturally through rain in the atmosphere. The erosion in limestone creates intricate caves that serve as storage pockets for freshwater. This, in addition to a sediment topsoil, trap freshwater underground forming an aquifer.

The Suwannee River experiences a geologic shift halfway through the river in White Springs Florida. As the river passes over the Cody Escarpment, a geologic feature created by former high sea levels, the top layer of soil drops down and is much closer to the aquifer. The river is heavily influenced by groundwater and the aquifer below the Cody Scarp, while above the scarp, flow is primarily driven by rainwater.
